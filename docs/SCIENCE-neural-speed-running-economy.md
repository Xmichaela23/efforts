# SCIENCE — Neural Speed (heavy strength for running economy)

**Status:** Citable grounding for the `neural_speed` protocol (`performance-neural.ts`). The *mechanism* — heavy, low-volume strength training improves running economy and rate of force development without adding hypertrophy — is **peer-reviewed and one of the better-supported claims in endurance S&C**; the *exact dosing* (the neural %/rep curve, the PAP protocol) is practitioner convention. Same discipline as `SCIENCE-5x5-linear-progression.md`.

**Why this doc:** the protocol cited "Rønnestad et al. and concurrent training research" **in a code header only**. This verifies the mechanism, flags the dosing, and records the true (supplementary) role.

---

## 1. What it IS + what it's FOR

A **supplementary strength slot for runners with barbell access** whose goal is **running economy / power**, not size:
- **Base** deliberately runs a *hypertrophy primer* (3×10-8 @ 65-72%) — "neural loading requires a hypertrophy base; jumping to heavy triples in week 1 skips adaptation."
- **Build onward** is the purpose: heavy low-rep lower-body neural work (~3×2-3 @ 85-89%, RIR 1-2), upper strength (~3×5-6 @ 72-82%), and a **Speed phase adding post-activation potentiation** (e.g. box jumps paired with heavy lifts).
- **Bodyweight/bands tier downgrades** the neural work to maintenance (no loadable resistance → no heavy neural stimulus).

True role (from the header): *"True heavy loading for neural adaptation and running economy … maximal motor-unit recruitment without hypertrophy (volume too low to trigger muscle growth) … it doesn't interfere with running adaptations."* **Supplementary** — a strength slot inside a runner's week, not a standalone S&C block.

## 2. The cited principle (peer-reviewed — SOUND)

- **Heavy strength training improves endurance performance and running economy.** Two reviews/meta-analyses establish this directly: Rønnestad & Mujika (2014) [the protocol's named anchor] and Beattie et al. (2014). Heavy resistance training improves economy/time-trial performance in trained endurance athletes.
- **The mechanism is neural (RFD / motor-unit recruitment / stiffness), not hypertrophic** at high load / low volume. Aagaard & Andersen (2010) on neuromuscular adaptation and RFD in endurance athletes; the NSCA load–rep continuum [ACSM 2009] for "≥~85% / low reps → neural, not size."
- **Explosive/plyometric (PAP) work improves running economy** specifically: Paavolainen et al. (1999) — explosive-strength training improved 5 km time via economy + muscle power; Saunders et al. (2006) — short-term plyometric training improved economy in trained distance runners. This backs the Speed-phase box-jump/PAP design.

## 3. What's convention (flag honestly)

- The **exact neural curve** (3×2-3 @ 85-89%, the per-week load steps) and the hypertrophy-primer→neural→PAP phase structure — practitioner programming, **no per-figure source.**
- The **specific PAP pairing** (which plyo, rest interval, complex structure) — convention; the *principle* (potentiation, plyo-for-economy) is cited, the *exact protocol* is design.

## 4. Honest grading

| Claim | Grade |
|---|---|
| Heavy strength improves running economy / endurance performance | **SOUND** (Rønnestad & Mujika 2014; Beattie 2014) |
| High-load/low-volume → neural adaptation, not hypertrophy | **SOUND** (Aagaard & Andersen 2010; ACSM 2009 continuum) |
| Plyometric/explosive work improves economy | **SOUND** (Paavolainen 1999; Saunders 2006) |
| The exact neural %/rep curve + the specific PAP protocol | **CONVENTION** — calibrated by response, no source |

**Bottom line:** **strong, well-cited mechanism** (heavy-strength-for-economy is one of the clearer findings in endurance S&C), **convention dosing.** A supplementary running-economy strength slot, not a standalone program.

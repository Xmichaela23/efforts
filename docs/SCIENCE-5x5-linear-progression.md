# SCIENCE — 5×5 Linear Progression (universal strength protocol)

**Status:** Science reference / citable grounding for the `five_by_five` strength protocol (the "get stronger / build strength" develop-posture, D-210). **Universal — the ongoing plan reads the body, never the birthday:** progression and deloads are driven by fitness/recovery signals, never age. The single exception is a beginner's **cold-start starting-1RM estimate**, where age is a legitimate input (a true beginner has no performance data yet); measured performance overrides it from the next session on, and age **never** gates the ongoing plan (see §5.5). (Broader masters/age *modulation* of the ongoing plan stays a separate net-new concern, out of scope.) Like `SCIENCE-concurrent-training-interference.md`, this gives **direction + guardrails, not a calibrated coefficient** — the athlete's own retest validates the exact load.

**Why this doc:** the strength engine can already prescribe real %1RM sessions with week-over-week progression and deloads (per the strength audit), but it has **no canonical 5×5 protocol**. Before building one, fix the parameters in the literature so the module isn't invented from intuition.

---

## 1. What 5×5 is (the structure)

5×5 is the canonical **novice/early-intermediate linear-progression** strength protocol. The defining shape, consistent across the source programs:

- **5 sets × 5 reps** on a small set of **compound, multi-joint barbell lifts** (squat, bench press, deadlift, overhead press, barbell row). 5 reps sits in the **strength-biased** end of the load–rep continuum (heavy enough to drive maximal-strength adaptation, enough volume to drive hypertrophy support) [ACSM 2009; Schoenfeld 2017].
- **2 sessions/week** (the **endurance-adapted develop frequency**), full-body, on **non-consecutive days**. The classic *non-concurrent* 5×5 is 3×/week [Rippetoe & Baker 2014] — but **concurrent with endurance training the develop dose is 2×/week**: a third weekly strength session competes with cardio recovery and amplifies the interference effect, so 2× *develops* strength while a third slot (if used at all) is **maintenance only** [Rønnestad et al. — 2× develop / 1× maintain; see `SCIENCE-concurrent-training-interference.md`].
- **A/B alternating sessions** — two complementary full-body templates rotated across the weekly slots (at 2×/week: a given week is A-B, the next B-A). Alternation balances squat/pull/press frequency and manages per-lift fatigue [Starr 1976; "Madcow 5×5"].
- The deadlift is typically trained at **lower set volume** (e.g. 1×5 work set) than the other lifts because of its disproportionate systemic/recovery cost [Rippetoe & Baker 2014].

## 2. The progression model (weekly %1RM ramp)

Linear progression = **add load on a fixed schedule** while sets/reps stay constant; the adaptation *is* the rising load.

- **Load band: ~70% → ~85% 1RM** across the linear block. Start light enough to bank technique and momentum (~70%); the block's productive work climbs into the **strength zone (~80–85% 1RM)** where maximal-strength adaptation is greatest [ACSM 2009 — strength favors loads ≥~80% 1RM / ≤~6 reps].
- **Increment: ~1–3% / week** (equivalently small absolute jumps — the classic "+2.5–5 kg / +5–10 lb per lift per week" for upper/lower). Weekly (not session-over-session) increment is the **intermediate** linear model — appropriate once the athlete can no longer recover between *sessions* [Zatsiorsky & Kraemer 2006 — small, frequent load increments; "Madcow 5×5" — weekly ramp to a top 5-rep set].
- **Ramp shape:** the canonical intermediate 5×5 **ramps the 5 sets within a session** to a single top working set, then increments that top set weekly — but the engine-relevant invariant is the **weekly working-load %** climbing 70→85 across the block; the within-session ramp is a delivery detail.

> The exact %/week is a **program-design choice, not a physiological constant.** The literature bounds it (too small = no overload; too large = premature stall); the athlete's retest (§4) tells us which end of 1–3% they actually tolerated.

## 3. Deload (planned unloading)

Linear progression accumulates fatigue faster than it can be dissipated; planned unloading restores the capacity to keep progressing [Zatsiorsky & Kraemer 2006 — restitution; Helms et al. 2015 — fatigue management].

- **Cadence: every 4–6 weeks.** (A standard 3:1 loading pattern — three loading weeks, one unloading — sits at the 4-week end of this band and is the common default.)
- **Magnitude: ~40–50% load drop** for that week (sets/reps held or slightly reduced) — deep enough to dissipate accumulated fatigue, light enough to retain the pattern. This is a **deeper** cut than a normal maintenance deload because linear progression drives fatigue harder.
- After the deload, resume at (or just below) the pre-deload working load and continue the weekly ramp.

## 4. The linear ceiling → retest (when linear progression ends)

Linear progression is **finite by definition** — load cannot rise forever on a fixed scheme. The block has a natural end:

- **Duration ceiling: ~16–20 weeks.** Beyond this, an athlete is no longer "novice/early-intermediate" on these lifts and weekly linear load increments stop being recoverable [Rippetoe & Baker 2014 — the novice → intermediate transition; progression must lengthen its time-base as training age rises].
- **Stall trigger: repeated failure to complete the prescribed reps at the scheduled load** (the standard rule: a missed target across **two-to-three** consecutive attempts at the same load, after a deload has been tried, signals the linear phase is exhausted) [Rippetoe & Baker 2014].
- **The endpoint is a RETEST**, not an infinite ramp: when the duration ceiling or the stall trigger is hit, the block closes with a **strength retest** (re-establish the working 1RM/5RM), which both measures the block's gain and re-bases the next block. **This maps exactly onto the D-213 non-race terminal shape: develop → retest.** A 5×5 block is a develop block whose terminal is the retest.

## 5. What the engine must encode (direction + guardrails)

The module is faithful to the science if it expresses:

1. **5×5 on compound lifts, 2×/week (endurance-adapted develop frequency), A/B alternating** (deadlift at reduced set volume).
2. A **weekly working-load % that climbs ~70→85% 1RM** across the block at **~1–3%/week** — anchored to the athlete's known/estimated 1RM, **never** a fixed absolute load.
3. A **deload every 4–6 weeks at ~40–50% load** (rides the plan's existing 3:1 recovery cadence where present).
4. A **block length bounded by the ~16–20-week linear ceiling**, terminating in a **retest** (the D-213 retest terminal), with a stall fallback (repeated missed targets → retest early).

**What it must NOT claim** (honesty, mirroring the interference doc): that the weekly % is a guaranteed gain curve, that linear progression continues indefinitely, or any age/recovery-tier personalization (that's the separate masters concern). The retest is the truth signal; the prescribed ramp is the hypothesis.

## 5.5 The 1RM anchor + the age boundary (two rules)

5×5 prescribes **% of 1RM**, so it must anchor to a 1RM. Two regimes, with a clean boundary:

- **Rule 1 — ongoing tuning reads the body, never the birthday.** The weekly % climb and the deloads are driven by the engine's existing **fitness/recovery signals** — observed performance (logged work → estimated 1RM via Epley/Brzycki), the plan's recovery weeks, readiness/ACWR — **never age**. The same block adapts to a 25- and a 55-year-old by their *measured response*, not their birth year. Re-basing the working 1RM from observed lifts is the "reads the body" mechanism; that is what makes the protocol universal.
- **Rule 2 — age is legitimate at the COLD START only.** A true beginner has **no performance data** — there is nothing to read yet. To set a *conservative, safe first load*, age is a legitimate input **alongside bodyweight, sex, and training history**, against population strength norms. This estimate is a **seed, not a verdict**: the **first logged session overrides it**, and from then on Rule 1 governs. Age informs the cold-start seed; it **never** gates the ongoing plan.

The boundary: **age in at the cold-start seed; body out for everything ongoing.** Anchoring source by source — measured 1RM (athlete-entered) → observed estimate from logged work → cold-start anthropometric seed (the only place age enters) → RPE/descriptor fallback if even bodyweight is unknown.

## 6. Sources

- **Starr, B. (1976).** *The Strongest Shall Survive: Strength Training for Football.* — origin of the heavy/light/medium 5×5 and A/B alternation.
- **Rippetoe, M. & Baker, A. (2014).** *Practical Programming for Strength Training* (3rd ed.), Aasgaard. — novice linear progression, weekly-increment intermediate model, the stall rule, and the novice→intermediate "linear ceiling" + retest/re-base.
- **American College of Sports Medicine (2009).** *Progression Models in Resistance Training for Healthy Adults* (Position Stand). *Med Sci Sports Exerc* 41(3):687–708. — %1RM load zones (strength ≥~80% / ≤~6 reps), periodized progression and planned variation (deload).
- **Zatsiorsky, V. & Kraemer, W. (2006).** *Science and Practice of Strength Training* (2nd ed.), Human Kinetics. — small frequent load increments; fatigue/restitution rationale for planned unloading.
- **Helms, E., Morgan, A., Valdez, A. (2015).** *The Muscle and Strength Pyramid: Training.* — RIR-based loading and deload/fatigue-management rationale.
- **Schoenfeld, B. et al. (2017).** *Strength and hypertrophy adaptations between low- vs. high-load resistance training: a meta-analysis.* *J Strength Cond Res.* — the load–rep continuum (strength adaptation favours heavier loads / lower reps).
- **"Madcow 5×5"** (practitioner program, widely documented). — the **weekly-progression** intermediate 5×5 with set-ramp to a top 5-rep set. *Practitioner source, not peer-reviewed — cited for the weekly-ramp structure, not as evidence.*
- **Epley (1985); Brzycki (1993).** 1RM-from-submaximal-reps estimation formulas. — the **observed** 1RM anchor (Rule 1, §5.5): re-base load from logged work. Already implemented in the engine (`compute-adaptation-metrics/index.ts:94` Epley+RIR; `compute-facts/index.ts:119` Brzycki).
- **NSCA, *Essentials of Strength Training and Conditioning* (4th ed.); population strength standards by bodyweight/sex/training-age.** — grounding for the **cold-start** conservative starting-load seed (Rule 2, §5.5), the only place age legitimately enters.
- **Rønnestad, B. R. et al. (concurrent strength + endurance training).** — grounds the **2×/week develop frequency** (§1): **2× develop / 1× maintain** weekly strength dose, an **8–12-week block** paired with a **~20–30% reduction in concurrent endurance volume**, and **non-consecutive** strength/endurance days to limit the interference effect. See `SCIENCE-concurrent-training-interference.md` for the full concurrent-training treatment + the two-build ceiling.
